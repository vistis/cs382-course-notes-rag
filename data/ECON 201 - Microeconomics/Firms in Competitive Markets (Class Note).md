## Glossary

- **$\Delta$**: delta; rate of change
- **LARTC**: long-run average total cost

# Revenue of a Competitive Firm

In a perfectly competitive market, the price remains the same regardless of the quantity demanded and supplied because there are so many buyers and sellers, and firms can freely enter or exit the market.

In a perfectly competitive market, $P = MR = MC$.

In a perfectly competitive market, $AR$ (average revenue) and $MR$ (marginal revenue) are the same as the price.

- **Total revenue**: $TR = P \cdot Q$
- **Average revenue**: $AR = \frac{TR}{Q}$
- **Marginal revenue**: $MR = \frac{\Delta TR}{\Delta Q}$. Change in $TR$ from an additional unit sold
- **Average total cost**: $ATC = \frac{TC}{Q}$
- **Average variable cost**: $AVC = \frac{VC}{Q}$
- **Price**: $P$
- **Quantity**: $Q$

Revenue rises by $MR$, cost rises by $MC$ (Marginal Cost).

# Profit Maximization

- If $MR > MC$: increase $Q$ to raise profit
- If $MR < MC$: decrease $Q$ to raise profit
- Maximize profit for $Q$ where $MR = MC$

Marginal cost curve determines firm's quantity in a competitive market. Hence, the marginal cost curve is the firm's supply curve.

# Shutdown vs. Exit

**Shutdown** is a short-run decision not to produce anything because of market conditions. If shutdown in the short-run, the firm must still pay $FC$ (Fixed Cost). For example, a shop would still need to pay rent despite not operating at all.

The cost of shutting down is revenue loss ($TR$). The benefit is cost savings ($VC$). Because the firm still pay $FC$. Shut down if $TR < VC$ or $TR / Q < VC / Q$ or $P < AVC$.

If $P > AVC$, then firm produces $Q$ where $P = MC$ or $MR = MC$.

If $P < AVC$, then firms shuts down (produces $Q = 0$).

**Exit** is a long-run decision to leave the market. If exit in the long-run, firms no longer have to pay anything.

The cost of exiting the market is revenue loss ($TR$). The benefit is cost savings ($TC$). Remember, $FC = 0$ in the long run.

Firm's long-run decision:

- Exit the market if: $TR < TC$ or $P < ATC$
- Enter the market if: $TR > TC$ or $P > ATC$

$Profit = (P - ATC) \cdot Q$ or $Profit = TR - TC$

Profit per unit $= P - ATC$

Loss $= (ATC - P) \cdot Q$ or Loss $= TC - TR$

Loss per unit $= ATC - P$

> Marginal with marginal, average with average

> We cannot know for sure if the a firm is making a positive or negative profit if we only know about their $MC$, $MR$, $P$, and $Q$. We need their $ATC$. If $P > ATC$ they make a profit, but if $P < ATC$ they make a loss.
