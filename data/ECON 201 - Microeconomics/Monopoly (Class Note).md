## Glossary

- **Monopolist**: the only seller in a monopoly
- **Price discrimination**: the business practice of selling the same good at different prices to different customers

---

**Monopoly** is a firm that is the sole seller of a product without any close substitutes.

Barriers to entry (the fundamental cause of monopoly) have three main sources:

- **Monopoly resources**: a key resource required for production is owned by a single firm
- **Government regulation**: the government gives a single firm the exclusive right to produce some good or service
- **The production process**: a single firm can produce output at a lower cost than can a larger number of firms.

A monopoly can manipulate the market price.

In a monopoly, the marginal revenue is less than price ($MR < P$).

Increasing $Q$ has two effects on revenue:

- **Output effect**: higher output raises revenue
- **Price effect**: lower price reduces revenue

The marginal revenue is negative if price effect > output effect.

Like a competitive firm, a monopolist maximizes profit by producing the quantity where $MR = MC$. They set the highest price consumers are willing to pay for that quantity. This means that monopolist often does price discrimination as they usually set the price at the highest willingness to pay from their consumers.

The profit of a monopolist = $(P - ATC) \cdot Q$.

The supply curve of a monopolist is not the marginal cost curve as with competitive firms. In fact, a monopoly does not have a supply curve. This is because a monopoly firm is a _price-maker_ rather than a price taker like a competitive firm. This means that $Q$ does not depend on $P$, $Q$ and $P$ are jointly determined by $MC$, $MR$, and the demand curve. Hence, no supply curve for monopoly.

For competitive equilibrium:

- $P = MC$
- total surplus is maximized

Monopoly equilibrium:

- $P > MC$ ($MC = MR$)
- deadweight loss

**Price discrimination** is selling the same good at different prices to different buyers. A firm can increase profit by charging a price to buyers with higher willingness to pay. It requires the to separate customers according to their willingness to pay.

A _perfect_ price discrimination is charging each customer at their willingness to pay. This makes the consumer surplus zero ($CS = 0$). Producer surplus is the same as the total surplus which is equal to the total surplus as if it was a competitive market.

Perfect price discrimination is not realistically possible. The closet thing a firm can do is to discriminate price groups.
