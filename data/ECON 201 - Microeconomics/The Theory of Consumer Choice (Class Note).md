## Glossary

- **Budget constraint:** the limit on the consumption bundles that a consumer can afford
- **Indifference curve:** a curve that shows consumption bundles that give the consumer the same level of satisfaction
- **Marginal rate of substitution:** the rate at which a consumer is willing to trade one good for another
- **Perfect substitutes:** two goods with straight-line indifference curves
- **Perfect complements:** two goods with right-angle indifference curves
- **Normal good:** a good for which an increase in income raises the quantity demanded
- **Inferior good:** a good for which an increase in income reduces the quantity demanded
- **Income effect:** the change in consumption that results when a price change moves the consumer to a higher or lower indifference curve
- **Substitution effect:** the change in consumption that results when a price change moves the consumer along a given indifference curve to a point with a new marginal rate of substitution
- **Giffen good:** a good for which an increase in the price raises the quantity demanded

---

The budget constraint curve is similar to that of the PPF (Production Possibility Frontier).

The slope of budget constraint equals the relative price of the good on the $x$ axis. $Slope = \frac{rise}{run}$, where $rise$ is the change in the $y$ axis, and $run$ is the change in the $x$ axis.

When income decreases, the budget constraint shifts to the left and vice versa.

Slope is relative to the $x$ axis.

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS) is the slope of indifference curve. $MRS = \frac{P_x}{P_y}$

The four properties of indifference curves:

- **Property 1:** Higher indifference curves are preferred to lower ones
- **Property 2:** Indifference curves are downward-sloping
- **Property 3:** Indifference curves do not cross
- **Property 4:** Indifference curves are bowed inward

A consumer is most satisfied when they choose the point on their budget constraint that lies on the highest indifference curve.

A change in income will shift the budget constraint proportionally (increase - to the right; decrease - to the left).

The change in optimum is dependent on the nature of the goods in question - whether they are normal goods or inferior goods.

When the relative price of goods change, it will bring substitution effect. The optimum will tip towards the good that has a decrease in relative price, or the demand of the good with a decrease in relative price goes up.

Income and substitution effects work in opposite directions on the good whose price does not change.

The new optimum will be influenced by the effect that has the higher impact.

The optimum as a result of the substitution effect is at the intersection of the original indifferent curve with the budget constraint curve which is parallel to the new budget constraint curve which is the result in the change of relative prices.
